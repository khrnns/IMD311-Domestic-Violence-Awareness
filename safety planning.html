PLAN FOR SAFETY

your safety is the main priority

What is Safety Planning?

With the support of a friend or family member, you may create a simple safety plan for yourself. You can also seek assistance from a support agency in developing a comprehensive safety plan. Every strategy is unique since everyone has distinct requirements. It's a good idea to update your safety plan if your circumstances change, such as if you relocate or end your relationship.

If you're creating a safety plan for yourself, you'll already have some solid ideas for what to do if you feel threatened. When there is violence where you live, one of these might be as simple as phoning a buddy. You may begin your safety plan by jotting down these suggestions. Keep your strategy in a secure location where no one else will be able to view it. You might want to tell a friend or other support person about your safety strategy. Let them know that if you feel uncomfortable at home, you may phone them.

Support services can assist you in deciding what measures to take if you feel unsafe. If you are dealing with sexual assault, domestic or family violence, the best place to start is by contacting a support organisation to create a safety plan.

Preserving Evidence

In the event of dating and domestic violence, the survivor should keep track of episodes, including dates, times, and witnesses, picture injuries, and seek medical help, among other things. Keep this information in a safe area where the perpetrator will not find it.

What To Do?

If you are still in the relationship
  • Think of a safe place to go if an argument occurs - avoid rooms with no exits (bathroom), or rooms with weapons (kitchen).
  • Think about and make a list of safe people to contact.
  • Keep change with you at all times.
  • Memorize all important numbers.
  • Establish a "code word" or "sign" so that family, friends, teachers or co-workers know when to call for help.
  • Think about what you will say to your partner if he/she becomes violent.
  • Remember, you have the right to live without fear and violence.
    • If you have left the relationship
  • Change your phone number.
  • Screen calls.
  • Save and document all contacts, messages, injuries or other incidents involving the batterer.
  • Change locks, if the batterer has a key.
  • Avoid staying alone.
  • If you have to meet your partner, do it in a public place.
  • Vary your routine.
  • Notify school and work contacts.

    • If you leave the relationship or are thinking of leaving, you should take important papers and documents with you to enable you to apply for benefits or take legal action. Important papers you should take include social security cards and birth certificates for you and your children, your marriage license, leases or deeds in your name or both yours and your partner's names, your checkbook, your charge cards, bank statements and charge account statements, insurance policies, proof of income for you and your spouse (pay stubs or W-2's), and any documentation of past incidents of abuse (photos, police reports, medical records, etc.)
