Understanding the impact of culture in our perceptions of domestic abuse may help us feel compassion and understanding for survivors, as well as gain insight into their experiences. Understanding the underlying cultural components of a domestic abuse dynamic may also help influence best practises when working with survivors. Counselors who assist with domestic violence survivors frequently hear from clients that they may have kept quiet about the abuse because they were afraid of being judged or being misunderstood by family or friends. And it's not unusual for others to say things like "why didn't she leave?" or "She picked him, and she decided to stay." What these terms lack is sensitivity to the cultural subtleties that underpin each victim's life experiences. It's possible that my cultural background, whether it's my religion group or my racial background's values, has nothing to say about divorce or separation, while religious views or cultural values in one victim's racial/ethnic group may vehemently oppose divorce or separation. So, while it may be simple for me to imagine how I would leave an abusive relationship, it may be a far more difficult option for someone with a cultural identity that is different from mine. Understanding the nuances of cultural variations gives us the opportunity to develop empathy and compassion for domestic abuse victims and survivors.

When we consider the mechanisms that engage with victims of domestic abuse, it is equally critical to be aware of cultural norms and distinctions. Taking the time to learn about a survivor's cultural beliefs may help police, counsellors, case workers, judges, and other service providers address a victim in the most effective way possible. Positive experiences in which survivors feel understood, accepted, and unjudged may make the difference in whether or not a victim seeks help to improve her safety. Finally, taking the effort to understand our own and others' cultural beliefs may be quite beneficial in the healing process. Especially for those who have been victims of abuse. It's typical for survivors to feel guilty about being in an abusive relationship and to hold themselves responsible for the repercussions of their abuser's behaviour. Analyzing the many cultural messages they may have gotten throughout their life, on the other hand, might help them recover by recognising how their cultural backgrounds and beliefs can often make it difficult to make judgments about their relationships and families. Working through these difficulties might help to emphasise that abuse is never the fault of the victim. Thinking about our culture can often involve weeding out harmful attitudes and bolstering beneficial components of our cultural identity that promote healthy relationships.

Common Pattern of Violence

An example:

A man abuses his partner. After he hits her, he experiences self-directed guilt. He says, “I’m sorry for hurting you.” What he does not say is, “Because I might get caught.” He then rationalizes his behavior by accusing his partner of having an affair. He tells her, “If you weren’t such a worthless whore, I wouldn’t have to hit you.” He then acts contrite, reassuring her that it will not happen again. But later he fantasizes and reflects on past abuse and decides to hurt her again. He plans on sending her to the grocery store, purposely choosing a busy time. She is then held up in traffic and returns a few minutes later than expected. In his mind, he justifies assaulting her by blaming her for having an affair with the store clerk. He has just set her up.
